# French Defense Playbook

This is the canonical GitHub markdown version of the French cheat sheet.

PDF export is optional and secondary.

## Core idea

The French Defense gives Black a clear center fight and a recurring structural problem.

- gain: `...e6` plus `...d5` and a reliable strategic framework
- cost: the bad light-squared bishop on `c8`

Most French plans are really decisions about how to solve, trade, or ignore that bishop.

## Solve the bad-bishop problem in the right way

### `...b6 -> ...Ba6`

Typical in Winawer-advanced structures. Worth real tempi because it removes the core positional problem.

### active Exchange development

When the center opens, the bishop can often develop naturally to:

- `...Bg4`
- `...Bf5`
- `...Be6`

### `...Bd7 -> ...Bb5`

Useful in some Advanced structures where Black wants to trade on a different diagonal.

### make it irrelevant

If the structure and endgame are good enough, Black does not always need to fully repair the bishop.

## Exchange Variation — branch early

### Plan A: Stonewall attempt

The Stonewall branch is tempting, but it only works when White cooperates.

Red flags:

- `Qh5+` or `Qe2+`
- `c4 + Nc3`
- `O-O + Re1`
- early bishop pressure on `d5`

### Plan B: aggressive `O-O-O`

Typical ingredients:

- `...Bd6`
- `...Ne7`
- active light-squared bishop development
- `...Qd7`
- queenside castling
- `...f6`, `...g5`, `...h5`

Practical rule: `Bg5` often just gives Black a reason to play `...f6`.

### Plan C: conservative `O-O`

Reliable setup:

- `...Bd6`
- `...c6`
- `...Nf6`
- `O-O`
- then `...Re8`, `...Qc7`, `...Nbd7`

This branch is less dramatic, but very stable.

## Advanced Variation — attack the chain base

Against `3.e5`, Black's core tools are:

- `...Qb6`
- `...Nc6`
- `...c5`

The point is to attack `d4`, not to admire White's space advantage.

In closed-center French positions, delayed castling is often more acceptable than in the Tarrasch or open Exchange branches.

## Winawer — positional boldness

After `3.Nc3`, `...Bb4` begins a strategically sharp branch.

Important themes:

- trade the bad bishop when possible
- value light-square control after bishop trades
- respect White's `Qg4` counterplay
- against `Ne2`, avoid automatic `Bxc3`; `...Ba5` and normal development are often cleaner

## Tarrasch and sidelines

### Tarrasch

- hit the center with `...c5`
- keep the position practical
- castle earlier instead of getting clever

### Sidelines

Against irregular setups, the right response is usually classical French play rather than a forced punishment attempt.

Typical anchor moves:

- `...c5`
- `...Nc6`
- normal development
- timely castling

## The opponent's main disruptions

The French is reactive. These moves change the branch:

- `Nc3`
- `Qe2+` / `Qh5+`
- `c4`
- `Re1+`
- `Bg5`

The main practical rule is to branch cleanly instead of trying to force one preferred setup into every position.

## Knight vs bad bishop ending

One long-term French reward is the favorable knight-versus-bishop ending.

Black often wants:

- stable knight outposts
- pawn placement that restricts the opponent's remaining bishop
- simplification into structures where the knight is clearly the better minor piece

## Fast summary

- if the center opens, activate the bishop on a real diagonal
- if the center closes, attack the pawn chain base
- if White disrupts the main plan, branch early
- do not force a Stonewall when the structure no longer justifies it
